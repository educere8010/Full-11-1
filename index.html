What Is a Clock?

시계의 본질 — 반복되는 과정

① We usually think of a clock as a physical thing, like an alarm clock or a wristwatch.

② But a clock is really a process embodied in a machine, and the nature of that process is repetitive.

③ A clock can be almost any process that repeats itself over and over again for an indefinite period.

④ Water clocks drip at a steady pace; quartz crystals vibrate regularly.

⑤ Indeed, it is almost impossible to think of a clock that does not depend on a repetitive cycle of events.

⑥ The only example that comes to mind readily is a candle marked in hours.

⑦ But here too there is iteration ― the repeated burning of molecules of wax ― so this too is an iterative process, although at first masked.

⑧ The use of radiocarbon dating is another, much longer scale clock that also appears to be like this.

⑨ It seems to yield a smooth time scale but in fact does not: the decay of atoms of carbon-14 is repetitive, although on a large scale it gives the appearance of being continuous.

우리는 흔히 시계를 알람 시계나 손목시계처럼 손에 잡히는 물건이라고 여긴다. 그러나 시계는 사실 기계 속에 구현된 하나의 ‘과정’이며, 그 과정의 본질은 반복이다. 무한정한 기간 동안 스스로를 거듭 되풀이하는 과정이라면 거의 무엇이든 시계가 될 수 있다. 물시계는 일정한 속도로 물방울을 떨어뜨리고, 수정 결정은 규칙적으로 진동한다. 실제로 반복적인 사건의 순환에 기대지 않는 시계를 떠올리기란 거의 불가능하다. 쉽게 떠오르는 유일한 예외처럼 보이는 것은 시간 눈금이 새겨진 양초인데, 여기에도 밀랍 분자가 거듭 타들어 가는 반복이 숨어 있어 처음엔 잘 드러나지 않을 뿐 결국 반복적 과정이다. 방사성탄소연대측정 또한 훨씬 더 긴 시간 척도의 또 다른 시계로 마찬가지인데, 매끄럽고 연속적인 시간 척도를 주는 듯 보이지만 실제로는 그렇지 않다. 탄소-14 원자의 붕괴는 반복적이며, 단지 큰 규모에서 연속적인 것처럼 보일 따름이다.

주제 (Topic)
반복적 과정으로서의 시계의 본질
요지 (Main Idea)
시계는 물리적 사물이라기보다 스스로를 되풀이하는 ‘반복 과정’이며, 연속적으로 보이는 것조차 근본적으로는 반복(iteration)에 기반한다.
글의 구조 (Structure)
  1. 통념 제시: 시계는 손에 잡히는 물건이라는 일반적 생각
  2. 반박·정의: 시계는 기계에 구현된 반복적 ‘과정’
  3. 예시: 물시계·수정·양초·방사성탄소연대측정
  4. 결론: 연속처럼 보여도 본질은 반복이다

Philosophy in Cognitive Science

인지과학에서 철학의 역할

① Philosophy allows us to ask much broader questions than many other scientific disciplines.

② It is capable of looking at the bigger picture and providing important insights into the relationships between different areas of knowledge.

③ Philosophy is particularly important for the interdisciplinary efforts of cognitive science, where it helps to bridge gaps between different disciplines and pioneer new ways for research.

④ Unlike scientific methods, philosophizing is a non-empirical approach that attempts to validate concepts through logical thinking and argumentation.

⑤ Philosophers tend to ask questions rather than provide definitive answers, and their contributions often consist of challenging established assumptions and proposing new research approaches.

⑥ However, for a more comprehensive understanding of the nature of consciousness, close collaboration between philosophy and neuroscience is required.

⑦ This means that while philosophy can provide valuable insights into theoretical concepts and broader ethical questions, it needs to be supplemented by empirical findings and experiments to reach a more comprehensive understanding.

Long-Term Media Effects

미디어의 장기적 효과

① In everyday life, most people think that media effects are things that show up during a media exposure or immediately afterward.

② Of course some effects do show up immediately, but other effects may take a long time to manifest themselves.

③ Let’s say you see an ad for a product on a website and you click on a buy now button to buy that product.

④ This is an example of a media message triggering an immediate effect — a buying behavior — on you.

⑤ But let’s say you did not click on the buy now button to buy the product; does this mean there was no media effect?

⑥ Perhaps, but also perhaps not.

⑦ If you continually expose yourself to ads in the media, you may gradually over time come to believe that you have more needs than you really have and that all of those needs can be easily satisfied by buying particular products.

⑧ This is a long-term effect on what you believe; it cannot be attributed to any one media exposure but instead gradually builds up in a steady drip-drip-drip manner over time.

Product vs. Process Writing

결과 중심 vs 과정 중심 글쓰기

① Traditionally, when teachers teach writing, they assign topics for students to write on; perhaps they do a bit of brainstorming about the topic during a pre-writing phase, and then have students write about the topic without interruption.

② Subsequently, teachers collect and evaluate what students have written.

③ Such instruction is very ‘product-oriented;’ there is no involvement of the teacher in the act or ‘process’ of writing.

④ In process writing, on the other hand, students may initially brainstorm ideas about a topic and begin writing, but then they have repeated conferences with the teacher and the other students, during which they receive feedback on their writing up to that point, make revisions, based on the feedback they receive, and carry on writing.

⑤ In this way, students learn to view their writing as someone else’s reading and to improve both the expression of meaning and the form of their writing as they draft and redraft.

⑥ Process writing shifts the emphasis in teaching writing from evaluation to revision.
